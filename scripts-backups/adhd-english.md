**[Opening — Personal Reality]**

Parts of my brain don't work the way yours do.

That's not a metaphor. That's not me being dramatic. That's just... the situation.

And I know that's a weird thing to open with. But I've spent a long time *not* saying it performing fine, burning energy I didn't have and I'm done with that.

So. Here we are.

---

**[The Glasses Analogy]**

Imagine living a hundred years ago. No eye tests. No glasses. You're born with blurry vision but you don't know that, because you've never seen clearly. You think the world just *looks like that.*

You trip constantly. You squint at everything. People think you're clumsy, slow, inattentive.

Then one day, someone describes glasses to you. "Wait — other people can just... *see*?" And suddenly your whole life reframes itself. Not failure. Not stupidity. Just the wrong tool, in the wrong century.

That's where I am. Except it's not my eyes. It's my brain. And the glasses exist. Just not in nepal.

---

**[The Biology]**

The part of my brain most affected is the prefrontal cortex right behind your forehead. It's basically the brain's project manager: planning, impulse control, attention, emotional regulation.

In ADHD brains, this area is underactive. And the main reason is dopamine.

Dopamine is the chemical that drives motivation, focus, and the feeling that effort is worth it. Most people have a baseline level of it that lets them do things simply because they *need* to get done.

My brain don't work that way.

Here's a good comparison: you can't fall asleep just by *deciding* to. Your pineal gland has to release melatonin first. You can lie there, eyes closed, willing yourself but until the chemistry cooperates, nothing happens.

Dopamine works the same way. My brain can't just *generate* the motivation to act. Without something that's interesting, urgent, new, or genuinely meaningful there's nothing. Not laziness. Not stubbornness. Just... an empty tank, neurochemically speaking.

Dr. William Dodson calls this an "interest-based nervous system." I call it exhausting.

---

**[A Few Symptoms — Just the Surprising Ones]**

I'll spare you the full list. But a couple of things that surprised even me:

I can sit down to read something important, and mid-sentence, my brain quietly opens a tab about something that happened three years ago and I'm *gone*. I wasn't bored. I wasn't choosing to drift. My brain just... left.

And the thing about "just try harder" — my brain actually performs *worse* under that kind of pressure. The prefrontal cortex, already underperforming, shuts down faster under stress. So the harder I try to force it, the less I can.

Also I can hyperfocus. Spend six hours locked into one thing, completely losing track of time and hunger and everything else. Which sounds useful, until you realize I have no control over *what* gets the hyperfocus. It's not a superpower. It's just... random.

---

**[The Masking Problem]**

People with ADHD look fine from the outside. Because we work very hard to look fine.

That mask costs a lot. And when you finally get home, alone it comes off. The exhaustion is real. The internal chaos is real. You just didn't see it.

So when someone says "you seem okay to me" what they're measuring is the performance. Not the person underneath it.

I grew up not knowing any of this. My teachers didn't know. The curriculum didn't cover it. We learned about scurvy and cholera and nothing about how brains can be wired differently from birth.

So for years, I had the vocabulary of failure — *lazy, unfocused, not trying* — and no vocabulary for what was actually happening.

---

**[What It Actually Costs]**

Untreated ADHD has measurable consequences. Not vague, soft ones.

Higher rates of depression, anxiety, addiction — often self-medication, because the brain is starving for dopamine and it'll take whatever works. Shorter relationships. Job instability. And statistically, a life expectancy that's significantly shorter — not from one cause, but from the accumulation: accidents, health neglect, chronic stress, impulsive decisions.

This is not a personality quirk. It has a real, measurable cost.

---

**[The Policy Ask]**

The medications that treat ADHD methylphenidate, amphetamine salts work by increasing dopamine availability in the brain. They're not new. They've been studied for decades. They're not controversial in the places that have actually reviewed the evidence.

A Nepali brain and an American brain are the same organ. The chemistry is the same. The condition is the same.

These medications can be regulated. Sleep aids are regulated — we didn't ban melatonin. We can do the same here. Control access, monitor use, create a proper system — fine. But *access* should exist.

Right now, it doesn't. And that's not a neutral policy choice. That has a cost — on real people, in real time.

---

**[Ending]**

I'm not asking you to feel sorry for me.

I'm asking you to build an understanding of what ADHD actually is because most people have never been given one. It's not about being hyper or distractible or bad at school. It's a neurological difference with real consequences, and real treatments, and right now those treatments are inaccessible.

I want to work well. I want to build real things, real relationships. I want to be a good friend, a good son, a good citizen — and I can be, with the right support.

This isn't a luxury I'm after. It's a medical need.

So. Now you know. What you do with that is up to you.
